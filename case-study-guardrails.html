What if the safest AI response
is also the most frustrating one?

There's almost no UX research on how refusal guardrails affect the experience of multi-turn AI conversations. I decided to find out — and found a tension nobody has a clean answer to yet.

Trust and satisfaction move in opposite directions.

Participants who experienced the permissive condition rated satisfaction significantly higher — but trust and perceived safety were the lowest of the three groups. The pattern reversed for strict: users felt safer, but rated the experience as less satisfying and less useful.

Learnings So Far

Still collecting. The goal isn't to confirm
the hypothesis — it's to let the data lead.

Designing from first principles is hard

No existing framework meant every methodological decision was a judgment call. That was genuinely difficult — but it also means the study is doing something that hasn't been done before.

Framing matters as much as the refusal itself

Beyond condition scores, think-aloud responses suggested that tone, explanation, and the alternative offered all affected how a refusal landed. This has surfaced a strong follow-on research question.

?
If the safest AI is also the most frustrating, what does that mean for adoption? Do users switch to less safe alternatives?
?
Does refusal framing — the language, explanation, and alternative offered — moderate the trust/satisfaction tradeoff?
?
How does this compound across a multi-turn session? Is one refusal recoverable? What about three?
Problem

AI safety research asks: is the model correct?
Nobody was asking: does it feel good to use?

Refusal guardrails are designed with safety in mind — but they're also a UX event. Every refusal is a moment where the user's expectation collides with the system's constraint. Whether that moment erodes trust or feels protective is almost entirely unstudied.

No prior research

Existing AI UX literature focuses on performance and accuracy. Refusal UX has no literature base.

No measurement framework

No established way to measure the trust/satisfaction tradeoff in a controlled study. Had to design from scratch.

No benchmarks

No comparison studies, no prior data. Every design decision in this study was a judgment call.

Research Process

A Figma prototype
standing in for an AI.

A live model would have introduced variability I couldn't control or reproduce across participants. A prototype gave me precise control over the one variable being tested: refusal behavior.

Three conditions — same scenario, three postures

Condition A

Permissive

Engages fully with the sensitive request. Minimal hedging. Prioritizes usefulness over caution.

Condition B

Balanced

Acknowledges the request, partially addresses it, then redirects with explanation.

Condition C

Strict

Declines clearly. Offers alternative framing. Maintains a firm boundary while staying helpful within limits.

Session structure

Step 01

Prototype design

Three Figma prototypes — same scenario, same inputs, three different refusal postures.

Step 02

Moderated sessions

10 participants, one condition each. Think-aloud while navigating, followed by structured debrief.

Step 03

Post-session survey

Trust (adapted TAS), satisfaction (UMUX-Lite), perceived safety, willingness to continue.

Step 04

Cross-condition analysis

Comparing metric scores across conditions — particularly the relationship between satisfaction and trust.

This study isn't trying to resolve the tension — it's trying to document it clearly enough that designers and AI safety teams can start having an informed conversation about it.

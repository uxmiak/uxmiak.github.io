Learnings

Turning the research lens
on ourselves.

Turning research inward

Examining our own team exposed bottlenecks we had normalized. Even research teams benefit from turning the lens on themselves — the same rigor we apply to users should apply to our own workflows.

Kano as a scope protector

Kano is often used to prioritize delight. Here it worked equally well as a scope protector — giving the team a principled, data-backed reason to defer features without seeming arbitrary.

Problem

Research was valuable.
But accessing it required a researcher.

The research team was producing good work — but it was locked in decks, drives, and institutional memory. Every time a PM needed a finding or leadership needed data to back a decision, they had to ask someone directly. That wasn't scalable — and it meant researchers were spending real time fielding requests instead of doing research.

Requests coming in ↑↑

PMs needed research before sprints
Designers needed context before ideation
Leadership needed answers for decisions
Engineers wanted to avoid repeated mistakes

Researcher bandwidth → fixed

Research Process

The instinct was to build a search tool.
But search is a solution, not a job.

My role in the discovery phase was to step back and understand the underlying needs across different user types — so the team would build the right thing, not just the obvious one.

Why a survey over interviews

The features were already scoped — I needed to prioritize them fast and at scale. A JTBD survey distributed widely was quicker and more defensible than pulling stakeholders into individual sessions.

Why Kano followed

Once the feature list was established, I needed a structured way to distinguish baseline expectations from genuine value drivers. Kano separated must-haves from delighters — and gave the team data to defer features, not just build them.

Same tool. Three completely different jobs.

The survey revealed that "find research" masked very different underlying goals depending on who was asking. Designing for one would fail the others.

User typeJob to be done
PMs & DesignersWhen I'm about to start a sprint, I need to know if we've already learned something relevant — without pulling a researcher away.
ResearchersWhen I'm scoping a new study, I need to quickly understand what's already been explored so I don't duplicate effort.
Leadership & EngineersWhen I need to defend a decision, I need a credible, summarized answer fast — not a link to a 40-slide deck from 18 months ago.

Everyone needed fast access to distilled insight — not raw research artifacts. That one reframe changed the entire product direction.

→ Summarization elevated from add-on to P1 feature
